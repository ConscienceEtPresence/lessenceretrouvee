The Diamond Approach

The path Almaas opened — presented and situated, without turning it into a dogma.

For Almaas, the question of essence did not remain an intuition : it became a practicable, precise, transmissible path. This path bears a name — the Diamond Approach — and a school, Ridhwan. It is presented here in eight facets, for the sake of understanding; the rest of the site explores the question of essence itself, which overflows any method.

IPsychology and spirituality united

Its singularity holds in one sentence : taking seriously, together, Western depth psychology (Freud, Reich, Winnicott, object relations theory) and the great spiritual paths (Sufism, Buddhism, Vedanta, contemplative traditions, Gurdjieff). Not one or the other — one through the other : psychological work opens the spiritual path, and vice versa.

IIPresence

Everything starts from an experience, not a belief : essence can be perceived directly, here, in the body — Almaas speaks of an “actual and palpable ontological presence”. This is neither an idea nor a metaphor : it is what the word presence designates throughout his work.

IIIEssence and its faces

True nature is not uniform. It gives itself in distinct qualities, which experience describes with astonishing precision — often by color and texture : red Strength, golden Merging, yellow Joy, black Peace, silver Will, Brilliancy… Each quality can be lost from awareness, and found again.

IVThe theory of holes

Almaas's most fruitful idea : when an essential quality has been lost sight of in childhood, it can leave a precise void — a “hole” — that we spend our lives fleeing or filling. Remaining present to this lack, instead of fleeing it, can become the door through which the quality is recognized again.

VInquiry

The central practice : an open investigation of immediate experience — sensations, emotions, thoughts — without a fixed goal, carried out with the intellect, the heart and the body. More than psychological introspection, and something other than formal meditation : a “journey without a goal” guided from within by what Almaas calls the Diamond Guidance.

VIThe body

Heir to Reich and to the Sufi system of the lataif, Almaas grounds everything in sensation : essential qualities are often perceived from the body — in the chest, the forehead, the belly — while also being able to circulate, fill the body, become a field, or open without limit. The body is not the obstacle : it is one of the privileged places where essence gives itself to be perceived.

VIIThe traditions, without the dogma

The approach brings Sufism, Buddhism, Vedanta, Taoism, contemplative Christianity and Judaism, Gurdjieff and modern psychology into dialogue — but it does not present itself as a combination of systems. It starts from direct experience, never from doctrinal fidelity. It asks for no belief; it proposes to verify.

VIIIThe Ridhwan school

Founded in 1977 to carry this teaching in small groups, first in California and Colorado, then in Europe and beyond. “Ridhwan”, an Arabic word linked to satisfaction, contentment and approval. The story of its founding is told here.

What makes the approach demanding

The word integration can sound gentle, almost consensual. In Almaas, it is more radical. It does not mean adding a little spirituality to a psychology of well-being, or a little psychology to a pre-existing mysticism. Integration means that every spiritual experience can be questioned down to its narcissistic uses, and that every psychological structure can be followed to the essential quality it covers.

This is why the approach can touch very concrete areas: shame, emotional dependency, the need to be seen, sexuality, ambition, defense, anger, idealization, fear of emptiness. None of this is excluded from the path. But none of it is taken as final identity either. Ordinary experience becomes material for truth.

MethodNeither cold analysis nor vague surrender

Inquiry holds together two qualities rarely combined: precision and availability. Precision prevents us from getting lost in vague impressions; availability prevents us from reducing experience to an explanation too quickly. The question remains alive until experience itself shows its structure.

EthicThe love of truth

The engine is not self-improvement, even if that may happen. It is the love of truth. This gives the work a very particular tone: we are not only trying to feel better, we are trying to see more truly. And seeing more truly sometimes means meeting exactly what the judge, self-image, or defense wanted to avoid.

“My interest here is not in psychotherapy. My interest is in the Work.” A. H. Almaas, quoted in John Davis, The Diamond Approach (2021)

What the approach is not

Not a psychotherapy. It sometimes achieves the same effects, but its goal is not to cure a symptom : it is to recover what we are.

Not a religion. No creed, no required rite, no membership : a practice of direct investigation of experience.

And this site is not its showcase. We present the approach because it is the work of the author who guides us — but the subject of this site remains the question of essence, which belongs to everyone.
