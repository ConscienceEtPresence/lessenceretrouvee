The Man

Hameed Ali, known to the world as A. H. Almaas.

Behind the pen name stands a quiet man. A. H. Almaas is the writing signature of Hameed Ali, born in Kuwait in 1944, who came to the United States to study physics. His is a mind trained in the rigor of the sciences — and this is perhaps why his spiritual work has that very particular quality: an almost experimental precision applied to matters of the soul. The pen name itself announces a program: in Arabic, almās means diamond — a consciousness clear, precise, objective, cut into many facets.

A childhood between two worlds

He grew up in a traditional Muslim Kuwait, in a religious family, while describing himself as not religious in his youth. His first search for truth did not come through doctrinal adherence, but through a scientific and experimental attitude. What he kept from that background was something other than imposed belief: the question of the sacred, simplicity, presence — qualities he would later associate with spirituality itself. Hence a conviction that runs through his whole work: spirituality belongs to no institution; it is a natural possibility of the human being.

Berkeley, 1971 — the encounter

At eighteen he left Kuwait for the University of California at Berkeley: a bachelor's degree in physics and mathematics, a master's in physics, then doctoral studies carried to the threshold of the dissertation — and abandoned. Not out of any rejection of science: physics simply did not answer the questions that haunted him. What is consciousness? What is actually present when we say “I”? It was the time when California was in ferment — the human potential movement, Esalen — and the turning point has a place and a date. In 1971, in Berkeley, the young physicist joins a group led by the Chilean psychiatrist Claudio Naranjo — the SAT group, “Seekers After Truth.” There, meditation, Gestalt therapy, self-analysis in the line of Karen Horney, the enneagram map inherited from Oscar Ichazo, and the work of the “three centers” drawn from Gurdjieff all come together. Almaas will later say what he owes to Naranjo: the discovery that psychological work and spiritual practice, far from opposing one another, can support each other. This intuition becomes the heart of his entire body of work.

The birth of a body of work

In the years that follow, Hameed Ali explores, experiments, takes notes. With Karen Johnson, his lifelong collaborator — and, in the very early days, Faisal Muqaddam — he gradually discovers what will become the Diamond Approach: a path in which the direct experience of essence works its way through modern psychology. He begins teaching in 1976, in California and Colorado, then founds the Ridhwan school in 1977 — an Arabic word he links to satisfaction, contentment and approval.

“I am both a complete human being and the transcendent timeless truth of the spirit.” A. H. Almaas, foreword to John Davis's The Diamond Approach (2021)

The writer

In 1983, he defends a doctoral thesis in psychology, oriented toward Reichian therapy — signed under his civil name, Abdul-Hameed Ali — in which the foundations of his thinking are already taking shape: the body, essence, and the way human experience is built. Then the books come, under the name Almaas: The Void in 1986, Essence, The Pearl Beyond Price… A body of work that today numbers in the dozens of volumes — the Diamond Heart, Diamond Mind, and Diamond Body series, the great work of synthesis The Inner Journey Home, the trilogy on love — much of which does not yet exist in French. This is one of the reasons this site exists.

What his trajectory helps explain

This biography is not background decoration. It helps explain the style of the work. Physics leaves a demand for precision: Almaas describes inner states as observable phenomena, with their textures, conditions, and effects. Psychology gives him a vocabulary for wounds, self-images, object relations, and defenses. Contact with Naranjo shows him that psychological work and spiritual practice can illuminate one another.

His Arab and Sufi background gives a sensitivity to heart, soul, subtle qualities, and the language of the diamond, without making him the representative of an established religion. That matters for this site: Almaas is not presented as an academic professor of philosophy or as a classical religious master, but as a writer and teacher of inner experience whose thought articulates science, psychology, mysticism, and direct inquiry.

To rememberA method born at a crossing

His contribution is not simply to add spiritual words to psychology. It is to ask how a psychological wound, observed with presence, can reveal an essential quality; and how a spiritual realization, if true, must be able to move through personality, body, relationship, and ordinary life.
