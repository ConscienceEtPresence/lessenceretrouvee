The question of essence

The heart of this site — and, according to Almaas, the heart of all inner life.

What this word means

Everyone knows those moments of being truly there — fully oneself, without effort, without a role to play. Then it closes over, and we go back to the usual version of ourselves, narrower, more mechanical. The question of essence begins in that gap.

For Almaas, essence is neither an idea, nor an ideal, nor an elevated emotion. It is what we actually are — and it can be perceived, directly, in the body, with the same certainty as warmth or weight. He defines it as a presence that is “real and palpable.” Not something to believe in: something to verify. It is this very realism that sets his work apart — a man marked by scientific training, without reducing matters of the soul to laboratory proof.

Essence, presence, Being… each term has its own entry in the bilingual glossary, alongside the original English word.

How we lose sight of it

No one stole it from us. It became covered over — very early, and for understandable reasons. The child remains initially open to certain essential qualities: strength, joy, will, love can be lived certainties, without yet being known with the discrimination of a realized adult. But as the body, memory, self-images, and object relations mature, these qualities need to be recognized, held, and integrated. No environment ever manages this perfectly — not through ill will, nor through fault: through simple humanity.

Wherever a quality went unseen, unsupported, or unintegrated, the child may cut itself off from it so as no longer to suffer its absence. And in its place, it builds: attitudes, strategies, self-images — what Almaas calls the personality, in close dialogue with modern developmental psychology. The personality is not the enemy: it is a necessary adaptive structure, capable of relationship and functioning, but it eventually imitates what it no longer touches directly. The work is not to destroy it, but to understand it finely enough for it to become transparent to the original.

Do not confuse

Essence does not mean a pleasant mood or an elevated emotion: it is ontological presence, experienced as real.

The child's openness does not mean adult realization: the qualities are close, but still need to be recognized, differentiated, and integrated.

Personality does not mean an enemy to be destroyed: it becomes problematic when it takes itself to be real identity.

Essence as substance, not as an idea

One difficulty, when first discovering Almaas, is that the word essence seems familiar. We think we understand it: the depth of oneself, the better part, the authentic dimension. But in his work the word becomes much more precise. Essence is not merely a noble name for inwardness: it presents itself as a subtle substance, a presence with thickness, density, sometimes color, temperature, and location in the body. It may be red and firm as strength, white and dense as will, green and tender as compassion, black and vast as peace. This language is not ornamental: it distinguishes essential experience from a mere emotion, opinion, or pleasant psychological state.

This changes the way the whole work is read. Almaas is not asking us simply to think differently, or to believe a metaphysics. He invites us to verify, in immediate experience, whether a quality is present as real presence or whether we are only speaking about it. We can have the idea of strength while feeling contracted and defensive; we can speak of love while trying to possess the other; we can want peace while fleeing an agitation that has not been understood. Essence has the character of lived obviousness: it does not need to convince itself.

DistinctionEssence and emotion

Emotion is a movement of personality and of the nervous system: anger, fear, sadness, excitement, relief. It has its own intelligence, but it is often reactive. Essence is not an emotional discharge: it is a stable, substantial presence that can contain emotion without being carried away by it. Essential compassion may allow tears, but it is not sadness. Essential strength may say no, but it is not defensive anger.

DistinctionEssence and subtle energy

Almaas also distinguishes essence from subtle energies. Energy circulates, stimulates, opens, or activates; essence is more fundamental, like the very substance of which certain energies may be expressions. The nuance matters: one can be moved by a great energy without becoming more real, more true, more free. Essential presence brings a quality of reality and knowing.

DistinctionEssence and emptiness

Emptiness is not the opposite of essence. Across several books, void or space appears as what is revealed when identifications dissolve. It may first be lived as lack, hole, absence of support, or disappearance of self. But when it is freed from the deficient belief, it becomes openness, space, availability. Essence is fullness; space is the openness in which that fullness can reveal itself without being compressed by self-image.

MaturationState and station

An essential experience may appear as a passing state: a moment of strength, peace, value, or love. The work does not stop there. A quality becomes a station when it is understood, integrated, and available in ordinary life. This is why Almaas stresses maturation: it is not enough to taste an opening; the opening must be allowed to transform the personality structures that prevented it from living.

Essence as teacher

From this perspective, essence is not only the goal of the path: it becomes its teacher. When an essential quality manifests, it illuminates precisely what had prevented it from being lived. Strength reveals strategies of powerlessness or hardness; will reveals passivity, rebellion, or control; compassion reveals self-hatred and the inner judge; value reveals narcissistic compensations. Each quality shows its reverse side, not in order to condemn personality, but to make it intelligible.

This is what gives Almaas's work its transforming power. We do not only learn notions: we learn how a notion becomes an experience, how an experience reveals a defense, how a defense protects a wound, and how the wound can become the entrance into a more real quality. Theory then becomes a living map: it changes perception.

The holes — the lack that shows the way

A quality lost from sight can leave a precise trace: an emptiness, a hollow that can be felt — where there was strength, a sense of powerlessness; where there was worth, the dull shame of not mattering; where there was love, a heart that feels dry. Almaas calls these the holes. We spend a great part of our lives fleeing them — through activity, possessions, relationships, screens — or filling them with substitutes.

His discovery, the one that changed everything, is disarmingly simple: the hole is not the enemy. Lack can indicate, in negative relief, a quality that has become inaccessible. If we stop fleeing it — if we let ourselves feel it, precisely, without filling it in — the emptiness can reveal its layers: old pain, defense, self-image, object relation, and sometimes the essential quality it was signaling. The lack was not merely an absence: it could become a door.

“The spiritual journey is not about having experiences, interesting insights, or unusual perceptions.” A. H. Almaas, The Unfolding Now, ch. 1

How it returns

Nothing to manufacture by force: essence is not an object to produce, but a presence to recognize. The path Almaas proposes comes down to a single stance — being where one is: staying present to what is being lived right now, including the uncomfortable, without correcting it or fleeing it, and questioning it with open curiosity. This is the practice he calls inquiry, investigation. Pursued patiently, it moves through the layers — self-images, the inner judge, the holes — and as they are understood, the essential qualities can reappear, each with its own particular flavor, then ask to be integrated.

This is why this site bears its name. Essence is not to be invented, nor earned: it is to be regained — because it never stopped being there.
