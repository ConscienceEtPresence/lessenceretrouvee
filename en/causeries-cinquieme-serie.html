Fifth series

Thirteen talks for letting go of the last supports: becoming poor in spirit, passing through absence, and living a true life.

The fifth series is the most stripped down. Almaas removes even spiritual supports: states, experiences, teachings, self-images, and the search for awakening. What remains is love of truth for its own sake, poverty of spirit, absolute absence, and the possibility of a life that serves reality.

This is not a spectacular conclusion. It is a quiet sobriety: seeing how the soul turns even spirituality into possession, then learning to know reality firsthand, without a position to defend.

The Movement of the Series

  1. Practice begins with a stripping down: no longer possessing one's spirituality.
  2. Suffering is explored down to its root in separation and appropriation.
  3. Teachings themselves must be released in order to know directly.
  4. True life becomes service of truth, attunement, and expression of reality.

01

Poor in Spirit

Source: Diamond Heart V, chapter 1.

The Starting Point

Almaas returns to the Beatitudes: poverty of spirit, mourning, meekness, hunger for righteousness. The point is not a morality of deprivation but radical availability.

The Turn

Being poor in spirit does not mean devaluing oneself. It means ceasing to possess: one's certainties, spiritual accomplishments, personal importance, even one's idea of realization.

Living Example

Poverty asks for love, devotion, struggle, nonattachment, and understanding. It does not manufacture a humble image; it makes the soul naked enough to want truth as it is.

First inquiry: what do I still want to possess in my spiritual life?

02

The Guest Arrives Only at Night

Source: Diamond Heart V, chapter 2.

The Starting Point

The talk meditates on a poem in which the Guest arrives only at night. Night is not only darkness: it is the unknown where the familiar self is no longer in command.

The Turn

The Beloved cannot be reduced to an experience the ego can summon. It appears when relational claims, images, and familiar lights are undone.

Living Example

Almaas lets passion and nakedness meet. Love is not opposed to emptiness; it becomes more intense when the soul stops protecting itself with knowing.

First inquiry: what familiar light prevents the night in which the Guest can come?

03

Sink Your Boats

Source: Diamond Heart V, chapter 3.

The Starting Point

Schools, practices, religions, and methods can support the journey. They can also become refuges, identities, or guarantees.

The Turn

Sinking one's boats means renouncing inner escape routes. One no longer keeps the path as a possession; one consents not to return to the old support.

Living Example

One may meditate, pray, inquire, and love truth, but without turning these into activities meant to obtain security. Maturity passes through doubts, fears, and remaining attachments.

First inquiry: what spiritual boat still keeps an escape route inside me?

04

Suffering and Its Cessation

Source: Diamond Heart V, chapter 4.

The Starting Point

We all want suffering to cease. Almaas does not treat this wish only as a psychological need; he examines it down to the structure of separation itself.

The Turn

Cessation is not merely relief. It comes when separate differentiation stops being taken as ultimate reality and experience reveals itself as belonging to one reality.

Living Example

Work with suffering can become a kind of informed optimism: not fantasizing that everything will go well, but knowing the truth that supports the whole of experience.

First inquiry: what suffering persists because I take it to be separate from the whole?

05

Absolute Absence

Source: Diamond Heart V, chapter 5.

The Starting Point

Almaas questions consciousness itself: can it cease, and what remains beyond pure consciousness or deep presence?

The Turn

Beyond inner experience, he points to absolute absence. Even the most subtle spiritual states are not the final word if the soul turns them into precious objects.

Living Example

The path makes a circle: it begins with the practical, passes through the Absolute, then returns to the practical seen as expression of the Absolute. Daily life is no longer separate from the ultimate ground.

First inquiry: what inner experience do I still value more than simple reality?

06

Beyond Consciousness

Source: Diamond Heart V, chapter 6.

The Starting Point

Consciousness can become subtle, vast, and deep. Yet Almaas keeps asking what remains when even this subtlety can no longer serve as an identity.

The Turn

Self-realization is also revealed as self-annihilation. The path does not only give something; it removes the categories, grips, and familiar forms of the self.

Living Example

What seemed to be progress can become a more radical loss. The soul learns that truth is not a gain for someone, but a reality without an owner.

First inquiry: what do I hope to gain where the path is asking me to lose?

07

Here's to You

Source: Diamond Heart V, chapter 7.

The Starting Point

The talk introduces an attitude of affection, appreciation, and celebration toward what is here. Inquiry is not cold: it looks at the present with tenderness and precision.

The Turn

Every problem indicates something not yet understood or integrated. Loving truth for its own sake requires questioning even teachings and spiritual positions.

Living Example

Saying “I am depressed” is not yet precise enough. Almaas invites the question: what exactly, where, how, with what texture? Later, even the system must be released in order to know firsthand.

First inquiry: what doctrinal position protects me from immediate experience?

08

Living a True Life

Source: Diamond Heart V, chapter 8.

The Starting Point

The Work is not there to produce a merely comfortable life. It speaks to someone who wants a true, meaningful life, not wholly determined by social values.

The Turn

A true life has a price: risk, difficulty, sacrifice, the loss of a special image. Truth cannot remain an inner luxury; it must touch the way one lives.

Living Example

Presence grows in a shared field where people meet tasks, relationships, and ordinary details with sincerity. True life is recognized by its embodiment.

First inquiry: what do I choose for comfort at the expense of a true life?

09

The Basic Fault and Resurrection

Source: Diamond Heart V, chapter 9.

The Starting Point

Almaas revisits the question of fundamental separation: the feeling that something is broken, faulty, separated from reality and from its source.

The Turn

Resolving this fault does not mean leaving the physical world. The world becomes more open, more real, more alive, because it is no longer lived as cut off from its ground.

Living Example

Shopping carts, ordinary details, and dissatisfaction do not necessarily disappear. But they can participate in a resurrected world, less closed by separation.

First inquiry: where do I try to leave the world instead of seeing it resurrected?

10

Christ, the Logos

Source: Diamond Heart V, chapter 10.

The Starting Point

Almaas uses Christian language here to examine what the figure of Christ contributes to the understanding of existence.

The Turn

This is not only a way toward transcendence, but a redemption of existence itself: life, Logos, and the fullness of the world as expression of the real.

Living Example

The theme of original sin or separation may appear in several traditions. Here, the emphasis is recovered life, not an exit from life.

First inquiry: does my path include life, or only an exit from life?

11

The Pattern That Unfolds

Source: Diamond Heart V, chapter 11.

The Starting Point

We often believe we are a person practicing over time in order to obtain something later.

The Turn

Almaas proposes another vision: what we call “my life” is also a pattern that unfolds. Awareness and arising can become simultaneous.

Living Example

Thoughts, feelings, movements, perceptions: everything can be seen as it emerges. Practice discovers the living unfolding instead of reinforcing the imagined pilot.

First inquiry: what scenario do I think I am steering while it is actually unfolding?

12

Attunement with Objective Reality

Source: Diamond Heart V, chapter 12.

The Starting Point

Almaas clarifies that the Diamond Approach is not abstract metaphysics or detached philosophy, but an experiential science of the soul, Essence, and the Divine.

The Turn

The function of the soul is not to maximize pleasure or self-image. It can become an organ of truth and service, purified by love of objective reality.

Living Example

Serving truth requires seeing one's ego, misery, and strategies. The soul becomes more able to act because it is no longer only trying to develop itself.

First inquiry: do I want truth in order to develop myself, or in order to serve it?

13

Divine Quintessence

Source: Diamond Heart V, chapter 13.

The Starting Point

The final talk distinguishes several forms of ignorance: what we have learned that veils, but also a more fundamental ignorance of real nature.

The Turn

True nature is at once emptiness, nonbeing, clarity, and living dynamism. It cannot be reduced to a single spiritual category.

Living Example

Silence, absence, presence, divine play, and unfolding can be recognized as one dynamic reality. The path ends in openness, not in a final definition.

First inquiry: can I let emptiness, presence, and dynamism be one reality?

What This Series Adds to the Site

It gives the journey its final depth: after notions, states, maturity, and integration, it shows how Almaas strips even spirituality of its securities. For visitors, this is a strong doorway into practice: loving truth enough to release what claims already to possess it.
