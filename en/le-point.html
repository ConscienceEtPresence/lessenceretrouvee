Door VI

The Point

The question “who am I?” carried all the way through. After The Point of Existence.

Narcissism is not what we think

The word is frightening, or amusing: we think of someone else, never of ourselves — a vain personality, caught up in its image, incapable of truly loving. That is a real sense of the word. It is not the one Almaas explores here.

In The Point of Existence, he gives the term a much broader, and much less accusatory, sense: a fundamental narcissism, present in everyone, including the most balanced, the most modest, the most seemingly loving people. This narcissism is not a character trait: it is the ordinary condition of any identity built from self-images rather than from a directly lived presence. As long as the sense of existing rests on a representation of who one is — a memory, a reputation, an idea of oneself, even a flattering one — it remains cut off from the Being it claims to represent. That cut, universal, silent, almost invisible because it is our normality, is the real subject of the book. It is no one's fault: this is simply how an identity is formed, in childhood, for lack of anything better.

The need to be seen

This fundamental cut almost never shows itself directly. It betrays itself in a thousand small, ordinary symptoms, so familiar that we never question them: the slightly-too-keen pleasure of being complimented; the disproportionate sting of being criticized or forgotten; that feeling, in certain rooms, of being transparent, as if no one noticed you were there; the need to tell, to show, to be confirmed in one's worth. None of this is pathological: it is the common lot. But Almaas invites us to look more closely at these movements, without judging them, as signals: each one points to a need far older and far deeper than the need for admiration. What we seek, behind flattery or recognition, is to be seen for what we truly are — not the image we present, but existence itself, as it is. And since, as children, none of us could be seen that completely, this need remains active, and replays endlessly in the small scenes of daily life.

“The longing of the human heart to know its true nature, our passionate desire to know who we are and to know our origin and our home, is all too easily placated with ready answers.” A. H. Almaas, The Point of Existence

The crossing

What happens if one stops, even for a moment, seeking that confirming reflection? Almaas is honest: it is not gentle. First there often comes a dull shame — not the shame of having done wrong, but a barer shame, that of not being enough, simply by existing. Then, if one keeps staying there, without grabbing onto a new image, a deeper emptiness can open up: a feeling of no longer knowing who one is at all, a moment without name or contour, almost vertiginous. This is exactly the point where nearly everyone retreats — into a distraction, a new story about oneself, a new quest for approval.

Almaas does not ask us to force this passage. On the contrary: he invites us to cross it in small touches, at our own pace, never rushing ourselves — staying a moment with the shame when it appears, neither fleeing it nor drowning in it; feeling the emptiness without rushing to fill it; returning, again and again, with the same patient gentleness one would offer a frightened child. No one crosses this in a single leap. One moves forward in stages, sometimes retreats, comes back. It is a path, not a feat.

The Point / Essential Identity

And on the other side of this emptiness, once one has stopped fleeing it: nothing spectacular, says Almaas — but something entirely simple, and of inestimable value. A true sense of identity, stripped of all image, of all story, of any need to be confirmed from outside: the bare feeling “I am,” here, now. Almaas calls it the Point, or the Essential Identity: a point of presence, precise and quiet, that needs no mirror to know it exists, because it feels itself directly, the way one feels warmth or weight.

It is not a conquest added to what one already was: it is what had remained, all along, beneath the images. Once touched, even briefly, this feeling quietly changes things: one still enjoys being seen, still appreciates recognition — but no longer depends on it in quite the same way to know that one exists.

Exploration — over a day

The small needs to be seen

A notebook, an ordinary day, and a little kind curiosity:

  1. Throughout the day, notice every time a small need to be seen or recognized shows up: a glance sought, a message checked, an urge to tell someone what you just did, a pang of disappointment if no one reacts. Simply keep count — a tally mark in the notebook — without judging yourself.
  2. In the evening, take two or three of these moments and ask yourself, for each one: what was this small need protecting? What fear, what loneliness, what quiet doubt about your own worth was it trying to soothe?
  3. Do not try to make these needs disappear: simply see them clearly, once. That is already, in its own measure, a moment of Essential Identity: being there, just as you are, without needing to prove it.

Terms for this door in the bilingual glossary: the Point / Essential Identity (le Point / l'Identité essentielle), fundamental narcissism (narcissisme fondamental).
