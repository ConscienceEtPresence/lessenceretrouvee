Third series

Thirteen talks for letting fundamental questions burn: why am I here, am I present, who am I, and how can I live without hardening?

This third series changes tone. Almaas no longer begins only with a lack or an essential quality; he asks questions that should not be closed too quickly. The talk becomes a flame: it keeps the mind from taking shelter in a borrowed answer.

The movement goes from radical inquiry to embodied presence, then to identity, meaning, support, boundaries, action, and vulnerability. The visitor discovers something beautiful here: understanding Almaas is not collecting notions; it is accepting not to know long enough for reality to show itself.

The Movement of the Series

  1. Fundamental questions must stay alive instead of being filled with spiritual answers.
  2. Real presence gives value and meaning to life before any accomplishment.
  3. Ideals, expectations, boundaries, and self-images become doors of inquiry.
  4. Being recognizes itself in spontaneous action and undefended human vulnerability.

01

The Flame of Inquiry

Source: Diamond Heart III, chapter 1.

The Starting Point

Almaas asks two simple and dangerous questions: why am I here, and where am I going? The mind wants to answer quickly, with a goal, belief, or project.

The Turn

Real inquiry is not first a search for an answer. It keeps the question burning. Conditioned answers — success, love, money, awakening, even “developing Essence” — can extinguish the flame if they replace listening.

Living Example

He shows how we borrow answers from parents, books, traditions, and teachers. The Work helps, but it is not there to answer in place of our own inner solitude.

First inquiry: what ready-made answer do I use to stop feeling my question burn?

02

Are You Here?

Source: Diamond Heart III, chapter 2.

The Starting Point

After “why are you here,” Almaas asks: are you really here? Not only your body, but you, in your hands, your cells, the room, the moment.

The Turn

Presence is not a state to manufacture. It is the simple fact of inhabiting what is. Our projects, hopes, and future conditions put us on hold and distance us from the very source of value.

Living Example

He evokes ordinary tasks, the body, thoughts, and the stories we tell in order to postpone being. When presence returns, daily life regains density, pleasure, and meaning.

First inquiry: what future condition serves as my excuse for not being here now?

03

Who Am I?

Source: Diamond Heart III, chapter 3.

The Starting Point

The question “Who am I?” does not call for a statement. Name, body, history, weight, image, emotion: all may appear, but none should be accepted without inquiry.

The Turn

Language traps us: as soon as I say “I am this,” the mind wants to turn a perception into an identity. The question remains because we do not really know; wanting to silence it becomes suffering.

Living Example

Almaas makes us feel how a simple self-image can become a prison. The point is not to obtain a good definition, but to let inquiry dissolve the identifications that take themselves to be “I.”

First inquiry: what “I am...” statement closes me most quickly to the unknown of myself?

04

The Chasm and Meaning

Source: Diamond Heart III, chapter 4.

The Starting Point

Every human being seeks meaning, often without knowing it. We believe meaning will come from a project, relationship, success, or philosophical explanation.

The Turn

The chasm appears when these supports no longer suffice. Almaas does not treat it as failure, but as a doorway: meaning is not mentally fabricated, it is discovered by falling into one's own nature.

Living Example

Even noble goals can hide the abyss. Perseverance, patience, sincerity, and compassion are needed so the emptiness is not filled too quickly with new scenarios.

First inquiry: what project am I asking to give me a meaning it cannot carry?

05

Essence and the Ego Ideal

Source: Diamond Heart III, chapter 5.

The Starting Point

The talk begins with goals, expectations, and the confusion between living direction and self-ideal. Even a spiritual goal can be an old image the ego wants to realize.

The Turn

The ego ideal promises an accomplished version of ourselves. Essence does not conform to that image; it reveals what is real when the pursuit of a perfect image is understood.

Living Example

Almaas shows that wanting to abandon one's ideal in order to attain freedom can still be a trap. The ideal itself must be seen: its promises, fears, and history.

First inquiry: what ideal of myself is disguised as spiritual aspiration?

06

Disidentification and Involvement

Source: Diamond Heart III, chapter 6.

The Starting Point

Many paths speak of detachment or disidentification. Almaas nuances this: we cannot truly turn away from what we have not yet met, felt, and understood.

The Turn

Disidentification is not cold escape. It passes through precise involvement in experience. Identification ceases when the truth of the identification becomes clear.

Living Example

He relates this to many essential qualities: joy, peace, strength, value, clarity, love. Disidentification opens access to qualities, but only if experience is actually traversed.

First inquiry: what am I trying to detach from before I have truly understood it?

07

Non-Waiting

Source: Diamond Heart III, chapter 7.

The Starting Point

We often live in expectation: of a state, an answer, a person, a healing, a realization. Waiting means that part of us believes we are not complete.

The Turn

Completeness is not spectacular greatness. It is simple, almost tiny: no gap between oneself and the moment. When there is no lack to fill, waiting falls away by itself.

Living Example

Almaas shows that even wanting to be complete maintains distance. When completeness is real, one is no longer preoccupied with it; one simply is what is here.

First inquiry: where am I putting my life in parentheses while waiting for something better?

08

Supporting Realization

Source: Diamond Heart III, chapter 8.

The Starting Point

A realization may arise and then not stabilize. Almaas insists on a neglected point: support must be integrated, not only a state attained.

The Turn

We seek support outside: mirroring, approval, recognition, structure. But essential support appears when the hole of non-support is fully lived, without being replaced by an external prop.

Living Example

The lack of being seen or appreciated can wound deeply. When essential support is integrated, it becomes possible to stand without constant dependence on the mirror of others.

First inquiry: what lack of support must I feel instead of compensating immediately?

09

The Dilemma of Boundaries

Source: Diamond Heart III, chapter 9.

The Starting Point

We spontaneously believe we are a separate person contained within boundaries. This belief seems obvious, but it organizes much suffering.

The Turn

A boundary is not only a fact; it is an experience to investigate. When Being is recognized, reality reveals itself without fixed edge. Love, gentleness, and humility become the breath of this boundlessness.

Living Example

Almaas is not asking us to deny the body or differences. He shows that the psychological boundary we identify with can melt into a wider presence.

First inquiry: what boundary do I take to be an absolute truth about myself?

10

Knowledge and the Good

Source: Diamond Heart III, chapter 10.

The Starting Point

Solving the mystery of life requires real appreciation of knowledge. Not only information, but knowledge that transforms our relation to reality.

The Turn

Ultimate knowledge reveals itself as ultimate good, because it discloses the nature of ourselves and things. But we do not jump to it by despising the steps: knowledge must be loved where it is given.

Living Example

Almaas links knowledge, truth, and goodness beyond the separation between good and bad. Inquiry becomes a way of loving mystery with precision.

First inquiry: what present knowledge am I neglecting because I want a grander truth?

11

Being and Understanding

Source: Diamond Heart III, chapter 11.

The Starting Point

Human understanding is both difficulty and possibility. The mind creates images and concepts, but it also makes liberating inquiry possible.

The Turn

Almaas's method becomes very clear: begin with a particular personality problem, understand its structure, then let the corresponding essential aspect appear.

Living Example

When the qualities of Essence are recognized one by one, they stop being merely isolated experiences. They become objectified, understood as facets of Being.

First inquiry: what personal problem might be the door to a precise essential quality?

12

Reacting, Being, and Doing

Source: Diamond Heart III, chapter 12.

The Starting Point

Who acts? What does doing mean? We think we must let go, disidentify, surrender, as though ego could produce these acts.

The Turn

Real disidentification is the cessation of a false belief, not a voluntary action. When reaction ceases, Being acts as a living flow, calm and dynamic.

Living Example

Almaas distinguishes personality's reaction from the spontaneous doing of Being. True action does not come from opinion or preference; it flows from presence.

First inquiry: what habitual reaction am I taking for right action?

13

Humanness, Vulnerability, and Being

Source: Diamond Heart III, chapter 13.

The Starting Point

Almaas begins with human meekness and vulnerability. Physically, emotionally, mentally, the human being is open, impressionable, woundable.

The Turn

We take this vulnerability to be weakness and build a shell. But our openness is precisely what allows us to be touched by reality, love, and the intelligence of Being.

Living Example

He contrasts protective armor with the image of glove and hand: when we stop taking ourselves to be the defensive glove, the inner source can move us.

First inquiry: what shell keeps me from being vulnerable to the intelligence of my true nature?
