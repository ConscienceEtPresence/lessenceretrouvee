Practice

Inquiry — the open investigation of experience, movement by movement.

What inquiry is

Inquiry : an investigation. Not in the police sense — hunting for a culprit — but in the oldest sense of the word : leaning in toward what is happening, right now, in the body, the heart, the thinking, without knowing in advance what one will find there. One does not summon a memory in order to explain it. One does not sit down to empty the mind. One looks at what is already there, as it is, and lets it say itself more precisely.

It is not only psychotherapy : one is not simply trying to heal a symptom or trace a causality back. It is not only formal meditation : one aims at no particular state, no particular silence, no posture. Inquiry borrows from both — careful observation, presence, discrimination, psychodynamic exploration — without being reducible to either. And above all it is not rumination : rumination circles endlessly around an already-known thought; inquiry moves forward, even by a few millimeters, because it stays glued to actual experience rather than to the story we tell ourselves about it.

Do not confuse

Inquiry: open investigation of present experience, carried by presence and love of truth.

Rumination: mental repetition around an already-known story.

Analysis: sometimes useful explanation, but one that can remain outside lived experience.

Formal meditation: a precious practice, while inquiry keeps a thread of discriminating exploration.

The three supports

Openness, curiosity. Not knowing in advance what one will find — and not minding. Certainty closes the inquiry before it begins; curiosity keeps it alive, even in the face of what disturbs.

Love of the truth. Wanting to know what is there, not what is reassuring. This support has a constant rival : the part of us that prefers comfort, the flattering image, the version that suits us. Inquiry asks that we prefer, every time a choice presents itself, what is true over what is pleasing.

Presence to the body. Inquiry is not conducted only in the head : it is felt. A tight throat, a hollowing stomach, an opening chest — the body often signals before thought has found the words.

The inner architecture of inquiry

In Spacecruiser Inquiry, inquiry appears as a vehicle: it moves because several qualities work together. Present experience gives the point of departure. Openness prevents us from reducing what appears too quickly. Not-knowing keeps open the possibility of a new perception. The living question gives movement. Discrimination refines what is true, what is reaction, what is defense, what is presence. And understanding integrates what has been seen into the soul itself.

The capacities that support inquiry

Inquiry is not a single mental faculty. Almaas describes a whole constellation of essential qualities that make exploration possible. Naming them helps us sense why inquiry can sometimes become dry, forced, vague or overly intellectual: a supporting quality is missing, and the exploration loses its balance.

Yellow — curiosity

The joy of exploration. Without it, inquiry becomes a solemn duty instead of a living adventure.

Red — strength

The courage to look at what disturbs and the ability to discriminate without collapsing.

White — will

A flexible, supportive perseverance, very different from the iron will of ego.

Green — compassion

The warmth that lets us stay with pain without turning inquiry into violence.

Black — peace

The precise silence that can dissolve a false belief without aggression.

Blue — knowing

Direct knowing: being intimate with experience before explanations.

Gold — truth

The taste for discerning truth and living it, not merely thinking it.

Clear — objectivity

The transparency that makes perception precise without making it cold.

Point — focus

The capacity to grasp the point, remain specific and go to the heart of the matter.

Pearl — involvement

Personal engagement: not exploring as a spectator, but including one's whole life.

Brilliancy — synthesis

The intelligence that connects the elements and lets unified understanding appear.

How to go about it

A guide in six movements — neither a rigid recipe nor boxes to check, but landmarks so as not to get lost at the start.

IChoosing the moment and the written trace

A quiet moment, where one will not be interrupted. And a notebook, a sheet of paper : writing slows things down, keeps one from jumping too quickly to a conclusion, keeps a trace to come back to. Inquiry is also practiced aloud, with another person; but alone, at the start, writing is the best support.

IIStarting from what is alive right now

Not a theme chosen in advance, not a problem one has decided to "work on." One begins with what is really there, in the moment one sits down — a tension, a mood, a phrase that keeps returning. The subject of inquiry is what presents itself, not what one had planned to examine.

IIIDescribing without explaining

The first temptation is to understand right away — to name a cause, a theory, a "why." We delay it. We describe first : where, how, what shape, what texture, almost what color. Direct knowing precedes knowing about — explanation comes afterward, if it comes at all, never in its place.

IVFollowing the thread without a fixed goal

What the Diamond Approach calls the personal thread : continuous contact with what unfolds, sentence after sentence, sensation after sensation — without deciding in advance where one wants to arrive. Setting a goal, even a well-meaning one ("I want to feel better"), cuts the thread at once : one then looks toward the exit rather than toward what is there.

VWelcoming what defends itself

The inner judge that comments, the boredom that rises, the fog that erases everything, the sudden urge to think of something else : these are not failures of inquiry, they are its next objects. We do not set them aside to "get back to the real work" : we turn toward them, exactly as toward everything else.

VIStopping when it breathes

Not when it is "resolved" — inquiry seeks no conclusion. The signal to pause can be that contact has become clearer, the breath returns fuller, or a weight lightens, even without being able to say why. But relaxation is not, by itself, proof of truth : it can also be a defense or an exit. We stop when experience feels sufficiently met, or when it needs rest. The rest will continue on its own, between sessions.

Guidance

As the practice stabilizes, many practitioners notice something Almaas calls Diamond Guidance : not a voice giving instructions, nor an occasional intuition, but a vehicle of discriminating knowledge in which precision, compassion, strength, openness and love of truth work together. It can seem to orient the inquiry movement by movement — without ever pointing to a destination. We speak of it here with caution : it is not something to summon or to wait for. It is, at most, something one can recognize afterward, when inquiry has been conducted without forcing : the feeling of having been guided, rather than having steered.

In French, the site keeps the deliberately sober expression Guidance Diamant, close to Diamond Guidance. What matters here is not ornament but precision: clarity, facets, discrimination and the capacity to cut through confusion.

The formats

Alone, in writing. Fifteen to thirty minutes can be a good doorway : enough to let the thread begin to unfold, not enough for the exercise to become a chore. It prepares inquiry; it does not exhaust it.

In pairs, with silent listening. This is one possible modality : one explores aloud, the other listens without commenting, without interpreting, without even prompting — their attentive presence alone is often enough to deepen what is being said.

Gentle regularity, rather than intensity. Fifteen minutes, several times a week, is worth more than a long, rare, demanding session. Inquiry is cultivated the way one tends a garden : through small, repeated visits, not occasional major undertakings.

Exploration — 15 minutes

What is alive in me right now?

Sit down, notebook open. Ask the question, once only : what is alive in me right now? Then write what comes — without sorting, without looking for the "right" answer. If nothing comes, write that too : emptiness, boredom, hesitation are already an answer.

Describe rather than explain. Follow what unfolds, even if it drifts away from the starting question — it is the thread that matters, not fidelity to the theme. Notice whether something relaxes, even slightly. If contact is clearer, stop there; if the relaxation feels like flight, name it gently and return to what is present.

Going further · after Spacecruiser Inquiry

The attitude of inquiry: openness and not-knowing

Inquiry has a central attitude that Almaas calls openness: a total receptivity to experience, with no fixed position, no preference, no judgment — not passive, but moved by the love of truth. Its paradoxical ground is not-knowing: recognizing that one does not know, not as a deficiency but as a quality of openness. For reality is inexhaustible — one can say an endless number of things about it without ever exhausting it, which makes the path an adventure without end.

Almaas then describes a force at work: the dynamism of Being, an "optimizing" tendency that, the moment we stop interfering, reveals true nature of itself. Inquiry therefore fabricates nothing: it invites. And as one advances, the effort of the beginning gives way to an ever more spontaneous openness — until the questioning itself happens without distance.
