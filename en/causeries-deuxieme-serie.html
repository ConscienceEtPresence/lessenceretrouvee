Second series

Twelve talks in which the effort to transform is itself turned around: freedom, no hope, acceptance, will, love, and being oneself.

This second series has a particular beauty. Almaas often takes the very desire for transformation — wanting to be free, to understand, to love, to change, to be oneself — and shows how that desire can become a new form of rejecting the present moment.

The thread is not to add a spiritual layer to personality. It is to see how personality can turn even practice into acquisition. Little by little, each talk moves the reader from an ordinary psychological question to a change of perception: freedom is not farther away, love is not an object, true will is not coercion, and being oneself is not obeying every impulse.

The Movement of the Series

  1. Freedom is not a state to possess, but the relaxation of fixation.
  2. Understanding is not primarily for feeling better; it must become implicit.
  3. No hope and acceptance reveal our ongoing rejection of the present.
  4. Will, love, and being oneself become expressions of Being, not strategies of self.

01

Hanging Loose

Source: Diamond Heart II, chapter 1.

What Almaas Takes Up

The appetite for liberation that appears after a taste of Essence. We have sensed something real, so we want total freedom, as though it were a state to attain and keep.

The Shift of Vision

Freedom is not first a particular essential quality. It appears when the mind is no longer fixed, when the heart stops grasping, when personality relaxes its grip in the moment.

Examples

Almaas points to a very ordinary, familiar condition: a mind not preoccupied, a heart not clinging. He also details factors such as energy, determination, joy, kindness, peace, absorption, and wakefulness, which mature into objectivity.

First inquiry: where am I trying to hold freedom instead of letting fixation relax now?

02

Mind and Essence

Source: Diamond Heart II, chapter 2.

What Almaas Takes Up

The word “mind,” which we often reduce to thoughts, mental images, or the brain. Almaas starts from this ambiguity and widens it step by step.

The Shift of Vision

Mind can mean the whole content of experience, then the space in which that content appears, then consciousness itself. Essence and mind are no longer simply two opposed domains: reality reveals itself as emptiness, presence, intelligence, and radiance.

Examples

He compares ordinary usage, Western psychology, Freud, Eastern traditions, emptiness, forms of consciousness, and Brilliancy. He also mentions the Sufi name “Hu” to situate the Absolute beyond color and consciousness.

First inquiry: am I taking my thoughts to be my whole mind, or can I sense the field in which experience appears?

03

Implicit Understanding

Source: Diamond Heart II, chapter 3.

What Almaas Takes Up

Our ordinary motivation: understanding so we will no longer suffer, working on ourselves to feel better, knowing Essence so we can finally obtain happiness.

The Shift of Vision

Understanding liberates, but if it is used as a tool for comfort, it reinforces the same self-centered orientation. It must become implicit: assimilated, metabolized, present as second nature.

Examples

Almaas takes up self-image, the desire to repair personality, and the subtler trap of becoming attached to Essence. Without Essence, personality suffers; attached to Essence, it turns even realization into a problem.

First inquiry: am I seeking understanding to change my state, or to let truth transform my being?

04

Attachment and Space

Source: Diamond Heart II, chapter 4.

What Almaas Takes Up

Attachment everywhere: to people, states, thoughts, dreams, objects, spiritual experiences, and even certain forms of consciousness.

The Shift of Vision

We think we want the object, but what we often maintain is attachment itself. The object changes; the glue remains. More deeply, attachment assumes an identity that is grasping at something.

Examples

Almaas takes attachment to a person, a car, a house, hatred, then traces it all the way to attachment to identity. The question “Who am I?” becomes a method for seeing each level of identification.

First inquiry: what identity am I protecting through this attachment?

05

The Teaching of No Hope

Source: Diamond Heart II, chapter 5.

What Almaas Takes Up

The great paradox of spiritual work: the more we taste freedom, the more the desire to possess it increases. Hope gets stronger precisely where practice seems to bear fruit.

The Shift of Vision

No hope is not despair. It reveals that the desire to be free can divide us: one part perceives what is, another wants something else. As long as freedom is desired against the present, it remains out of reach.

Examples

Almaas describes the student who gains understanding and then becomes more acquisitive about Essence. He also compares the inner attitude of war to a political strategy that claims to want peace by strengthening weapons.

First inquiry: what does my spiritual hope refuse to meet in present experience?

06

Acceptance

Source: Diamond Heart II, chapter 6.

What Almaas Takes Up

The fundamental “no” of personality: we reject what we feel, then reject our rejection, then try to get rid of the conflict.

The Shift of Vision

Acceptance is not resignation, nor a technique for making attachment disappear. It is an attitude of non-rejection. When what is is accepted, release follows naturally, without being made into a goal.

Examples

Almaas goes back before the parental superego: he speaks of the infant's autonomous regulation, organismic confidence, projection, and the progressive separation from felt unity.

First inquiry: can I feel the “no” that comes before my effort to become calmer, more open, or more spiritual?

07

Change and Truth

Source: Diamond Heart II, chapter 7.

What Almaas Takes Up

Very concrete reactions in a group: wanting to protect newcomers, feeling superior, not listening, being irritated, preferring to be elsewhere, wanting to be perfect.

The Shift of Vision

We want to change, but according to our old ideas of what change should be. Those ideas belong to our current condition. Real change turns over the whole frame, not just a few behaviors.

Examples

Almaas lets students' comments reveal the mechanism: each person comments on reality from an implicit claim to know how things should go. Then he brings the inquiry back to the truth of immediate experience.

First inquiry: what truth am I refusing because it would unsettle my very idea of change?

08

Will

Source: Diamond Heart II, chapter 8.

What Almaas Takes Up

Our usual idea of will: making something happen, pushing, correcting experience, moving life toward where we think it should be.

The Shift of Vision

True will is almost the opposite of this coercion. It is surrender to what is being experienced now, attunement to the natural order. False will begins as soon as we say no to experience.

Examples

Almaas takes romantic dissatisfaction, the effort to fill holes, and social pressure based on goals, plans, and projection. Staying with truth can feel as if one is moving against the whole cultural current.

First inquiry: does my will support the reality of what is, or is it trying to drag my experience elsewhere?

09

Self and Selflessness

Source: Diamond Heart II, chapter 9.

What Almaas Takes Up

The ordinary perspective of self: an island with needs, desires, rights, projects, preferences, wounds, and strategies of protection.

The Shift of Vision

Selflessness is not self-hatred. It is the end of the automatic centrality of the island. When this perspective relaxes, others are no longer seen only in terms of what they give to or take from “me.”

Examples

Almaas develops the image of islands at length: war, friendship, helping, disappointment, all can remain organized around the self's point of view. He ends by showing that openness allows spontaneous action, not passivity.

First inquiry: how does my island color the way I see the other islands?

10

Love

Source: Diamond Heart II, chapter 10.

What Almaas Takes Up

The word “love,” used everywhere, often without precision. We mostly think of an emotion toward a special person, in a special relationship, under certain conditions.

The Shift of Vision

Love is not primarily an object that we give or receive. It is the radiance of harmony throughout the whole organism. When love is real, it unites without erasing singularity: one can be fully oneself and not separate.

Examples

Almaas examines “I love you,” the conditions placed on the other, the need to be loved, and then love as overflow. He shows that a loving person does not possess love: they become a channel of its action.

First inquiry: where do I reduce love to a need, a preference, or a condition?

11

Friend and Lover

Source: Diamond Heart II, chapter 11.

What Almaas Takes Up

A student's question about friendship. Almaas begins with the ordinary friend: someone to chat with, who supports us, agrees with us, and helps us not feel alone.

The Shift of Vision

Friend and lover are not only relational roles. They reveal inner capacities: objectivity, trust, vulnerability, open heart, and complete participation in existence.

Examples

He distinguishes friend, intimate partner, and lover, then shows that the lover exists in us. The lover celebrates existence, loves life, truth, understanding, and is not trying to get somewhere else.

First inquiry: am I using relationship not to be alone, or to let the friend and lover appear in me?

12

Being Oneself

Source: Diamond Heart II, chapter 12.

What Almaas Takes Up

The most obvious and most mysterious phrase: “I want to be myself.” Everyone thinks they know what this means, but the “self” becomes elusive as soon as we really look.

The Shift of Vision

Being oneself is not simply expressing anger, love, sadness, or desire. It is discovering the precise truth that is relevant for one's soul now, without turning one truth into a law for everyone.

Examples

Almaas questions personal desires, impersonal spiritual ideals, the ego treated as only a problem, and the need to be personal in one's truth. For one person the relevant truth may be value; for another, compassion.

First inquiry: what truth is relevant for me now, rather than for the self-image I want to defend?

What This Series Adds to the Site

It gives visitors a more transformative passage: not only “here are the notions,” but “here is how a notion turns around the way I live.” This is especially valuable for someone discovering Almaas without wanting to remain at a purely intellectual level.
