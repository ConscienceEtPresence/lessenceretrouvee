Step IV

Essence and its faces

What returns is not a uniform light — it is a palette. Strength, Joy, Peace, Will, love that melts, compassion, the intelligence that synthesizes : seven qualities, seven flavors.

A light with many shades

Open any of Almaas's texts devoted to this subject, and the same surprise returns : essence is never described there as a uniform flow, a simple “sense of being present”. It is described color by color, texture by texture — almost as a mineralogist would catalogue a collection of stones. Dense red on one side, melting gold on the other, deep black, bright silver, tender green. This precision is not a literary manner of speaking : it is, his students say, what one actually encounters once one stops thinking interiority and begins to feel it. Each quality has its own density, its own temperature, its own place in the body — as distinct from one another as one scent is from another.

A kinship with the Sufi lataif

Almaas does not start from nothing. In Spacecruiser Inquiry, he explicitly draws on an older map : the Sufi lataif ("subtleties," in Arabic), five centers traditionally each associated with a color and with what he calls a sacred impulse — the yellow of joy (“I wish”), the red of strength (“I can”), the white of perseverance (“I want”), the black of perceiving beyond appearances (“I perceive”), the green of loving-kindness (“I am”). He does not copy this scheme as it stands — the Diamond Approach draws its own map, broader, with qualities the Sufis do not name in the same way — but he keeps its central intuition : the heart is not a single organ. It is a constellation of organs, each capable of its own perception.

The Red Strength

Red, dense, warm — almost an ember. Where the ego ideal of the Enneagram's eighth type hardens into a shell (“I must dominate, I must never bend”), the quality it clumsily imitates is far simpler : a quiet power that needs to convince no one. It is felt most often in the belly, the legs, sometimes all along the spine — a foundation, more than a fist. It is not the aggression that strikes to defend itself : it is what remains standing without needing to strike.

What blocks it, most often, is the fear of one's own intensity — the childish idea that being strong necessarily means hurting, overflowing, being “too much”. So we smother it, or mimic it through hardness, which is not the same thing. When it returns, day to day, it makes no noise : it is the yes and the no that come out without hesitation, the boundary set without having to raise one's voice, the capacity to stay present in a difficult situation without stiffening or fleeing.

The Merging Gold — Love That Melts

Liquid gold, warm, with no sharp edge. Almaas connects it to the infant's very first experience in its mother's arms — that developmental moment in which psychologists such as Margaret Mahler locate a union not yet separated : not “two who love each other”, but “a single continuum of warmth”, felt most often in the chest, as a soft melting rather than a heartbeat. This is the aspect the Enneagram's second type desperately tries to produce in another person — being loved to the point of merging — without knowing that it can first be given to oneself.

What blocks it : the fear of losing oneself in another, often inherited from an old merging that went wrong — too invasive, or broken off too soon. So we keep our distance, or cling out of lack, which is likewise not the same thing. When it returns, it asks for no partner : it is that quality of closeness one can feel alone, in contact with a tree, an animal, a tender memory — the boundary softening without disappearing.

The Yellow Joy

Yellow, sparkling, light — the Sufi lataif associates this color with a very simple impulse : “I wish”, the pure movement toward what draws us, prior to any calculation. It is felt often high in the chest or just beneath the sternum, like small bubbles rising. This is not the excitement that needs an event to exist : it is a sparkle that can settle on anything at all — a ray of light on a table, a well-chosen word, the simple fact of being alive on an ordinary Tuesday.

What blocks it : the seriousness learned very early, the idea that joy would be childish, superficial, or that it would invite misfortune if indulged too much. Many capable adults carry this block without even naming it. When joy returns, it is recognized by a precise sign : a laugh that escapes before we decided to laugh, a lightness in the body with no particular reason — and none needed.

The Black Peace

Black — which always surprises those who discover this map for the first time, since black is elsewhere associated with grief or fear. Here, it is the reverse : a velvety, thick black, deep as a moonless sky, associated in the Sufi lataif with the faculty of perceiving beyond appearances (“I perceive”). It is often felt as a stillness that encompasses the whole body — not a precise point, but a quiet density that needs nothing resolved.

What blocks it is the very common confusion between calm and the emptiness that frightens : we associate the stopping of inner movement with danger — boredom, insignificance, sometimes even death. So we keep ourselves agitated so as never to meet it. When it returns, day to day, it is that rare capacity to do nothing without being anxious about doing nothing : a silence shared in a room, a wait without impatience, a sleepless night passed through without struggle.

The Will

Here the sources waver, and that is telling in itself : the Sufi map of the five lataif locates perseverance in white (“I want”), whereas in Keys to the Enneagram, discussing the sixth type's fixation, Almaas describes personal will as a silver presence — supple, alive, shimmering, almost liquid. The exact name of the hue matters little : what counts is the contrast he draws with what personality manufactures in its place — an iron will, rigid, brittle, held together by clenching rather than by trust.

It is often felt along the body's central axis, like an inner column that requires no effort to stay upright. What blocks it : the conviction that we would collapse without external structure — a rule, an authority, an imposed discipline. It returns in moments when a commitment is kept without forcing oneself, when a decision is made without endless hesitation : a firmness that has nothing hard about it.

The Green Compassion

Green, tender, a little cool — the heart's lataif, associated with the barest impulse of all : “I am”, the simple kindness of being present to what suffers, without needing to fix it. It is felt most often at the center of the chest, like sap rising gently rather than a wave of emotion. This is not pity, which looks down on the other : it is a warmth that settles at the same level.

What blocks it : protective cynicism, or its opposite — a surface kindness that avoids real contact for fear of being overwhelmed. It returns, day to day, as that simple tenderness one can suddenly feel for a stranger passed in the street, for a difficult colleague, or — most often forgotten — for oneself, at the very moment one has just failed.

Brilliancy

Almost colorless — which makes it hard to place alongside the other six. Almaas once called it “the essence of essence”, because it contains, fused together and undifferentiated, all the preceding qualities at once. In Brilliancy, he describes it as a fine, luminous substance, circulating along the body's axis — less a color than a clarity. It is what produces the sudden synthesis, the flash in which scattered elements reveal that they had always been a single pattern : intelligence not as a score, but as a presence.

What blocks it most often, according to the working sessions reported in the book, is an old projection onto a father figure : the source of intelligence is placed outside oneself, in a father judged brilliant or disappointing, and one doubts having any of one's own. When it returns, it is recognized by a very precise sensation : the feeling that nothing more is missing, that a solution found has an elegant obviousness — and the curious disappearance of the need to check that one is right.

Exploration — to close the step

The quality that is missing, right now

Return to the seven faces above, slowly, one by one.

  1. Which one, at this moment in your life, seems most absent — the one whose lack is most felt, not the one you “should” have? Don't search for the right answer : note the first one that comes to you.
  2. Go back to a moment — even an old one, even brief — when this quality was unmistakably present. Not a thought about it : a precise memory, with a place, a light, a body that held it.
  3. Close your eyes, and look for it now as a sensation, not as an idea. Where, in the body, could it settle if it returned? Don't force it : simply open the space, and watch what presents itself there.

Nothing spectacular is supposed to “happen”. The simple act of searching, honestly, in the body rather than in the head, is already the exercise.

The lataif: the organs of subtle perception

Why speak of colours for qualities of Being? Almaas takes up an old Sufi map: the lataif (singular latifa), centres of subtle perception located deeper than the chakras — the "organs" through which essence is perceived. Their name states their nature: qualities of great fineness, the first subtle manifestation of essence. The tradition he cites distinguishes five:

Qalb (the heart), yellow — joy, contentment; Rouh (spirit), red — strength, the fire of essence; Sirr (the secret), white — will, support; Khafi (the hidden), brilliant black — peace, intuition, objective understanding; Akhfa (the more hidden), emerald green — compassion and loving-kindness.

The colours are therefore not decorative: they name a precise perceptual experience, through which each face of essence lets itself be recognized in the body.
