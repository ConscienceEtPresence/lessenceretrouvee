First series

Seventeen talks for learning to see differently: covered Essence, holes, truth, maturity, and the living work of a school.

This first series follows the talks in their source order. Its aim is not to replace the reading, but to convey Almaas's gesture: he begins from a concrete situation, shifts our ordinary interpretation, and reveals the essential quality hidden behind the problem.

The series is therefore not a simple introduction. It already lays out much of the grammar of the Diamond Approach: personality, Essence, holes, truth, compassion, trust, struggle, allowing, maturity, relationship to the teaching, and living transmission.

The Movement of the Series

  1. Essence is primary, but it becomes covered by personality.
  2. Lacks and desires reveal essential qualities lost from view.
  3. Truth requires precision, compassion, trust, and real struggle.
  4. Practice matures into responsibility, impeccability, curiosity, and transmission.

01

In the World but Not of It

Diamond Heart I, chapter 1.

What Almaas Takes Up

Ordinary life: career, independence, relationship, the wish to be oneself, the wish for loving fusion. He does not dismiss them and does not ask us to leave the world.

The Shift of Vision

These worldly desires become readable as distorted reflections of essential reality. Independence seeks the Personal Essence; loving fusion seeks a quality of love or unity already present as Essence.

Examples

He takes self-assertion, success, work, intimate relationship, and the desire for merging, then shows that the issue is not having these desires but believing their outer object is the real source.

First inquiry: in what I pursue in the world, what deeper quality am I really seeking?

02

The Theory of Holes

Diamond Heart I, chapter 2.

What Almaas Takes Up

Lack, emotional dependency, the end of a relationship, the need to be appreciated, recognized, or loved.

The Shift of Vision

A hole is not merely a psychological wound. It is the trace of an essential quality lost from view. When a relationship fills that hole, its loss feels like losing a part of oneself.

Examples

He focuses on value: when another person's appreciation disappears, the sense of worthlessness may reveal a value hole and then open into essential value itself.

First inquiry: what repeated desire indicates a hole asking to be seen rather than filled?

03

The Diamond Approach to the Work

Diamond Heart I, chapter 3.

What Almaas Takes Up

The difficulty of spiritual work: lack of will, fear of commitment, inability to surrender, and patterns one cannot simply change.

The Shift of Vision

He does not simply blame the student. He shows that will, commitment, and surrender are often blocked by unconscious fears. Psychological precision becomes a spiritual ally.

Examples

He points to will paralyzed by deep fear, commitment felt as loss of freedom or identity, and moral injunctions that fail because they do not reach the unconscious root.

First inquiry: if I cannot commit, what unconscious fear is my lack of commitment protecting?

04

Faith and Commitment

Diamond Heart I, chapter 4.

What Almaas Takes Up

The confusion between belief, faith, direct knowing, and staying committed when essential experience is no longer immediately present.

The Shift of Vision

Real faith is not based on abstract belief but on tasted knowledge. It becomes a bridge between a past direct experience and a knowledge not yet stabilized.

Examples

He distinguishes mental belief, faith born of belief, faith born of knowledge, and stable knowing. Commitment is linked to fear of losing the familiar identity.

First inquiry: what do I merely believe, and what do I already know from experience, however faintly?

05

Nobility and Suffering

Diamond Heart I, chapter 5.

What Almaas Takes Up

Suffering, attachment to outer pleasures, and the fear that living for truth would mean losing ordinary life.

The Shift of Vision

He distinguishes ignoble and noble suffering. The first comes from seeking substitutes for Essence; the second accompanies letting go of the attachments that block the realization of Personal Essence.

Examples

He mentions attachment to partner, work, money, body, home, and comfort, then shows that they can be filled by Essence instead of serving as replacements for it.

First inquiry: what suffering comes from attachment, and what suffering comes from beginning to be faithful to truth?

06

Value

Diamond Heart I, chapter 6.

What Almaas Takes Up

The sense that life has or lacks meaning, self-esteem, superego value, and learned standards of success or failure.

The Shift of Vision

Value does not come from inner superego approval or outer recognition. It can reveal itself as an intrinsic presence, dense, felt, almost substantial.

Examples

The discussion begins with student reports: meaning, freedom, strength, glory, joy. Almaas traces these experiences to essential value rather than a favorable opinion about oneself.

First inquiry: does my value depend on approval, or can I sense what is precious in me before judgment?

07

Truth and Compassion

Diamond Heart I, chapter 7.

What Almaas Takes Up

Our habit of choosing happiness, comfort, or a pleasant self-image over truth.

The Shift of Vision

Compassion is not only the wish to relieve pain. It is what allows us to tolerate the pain of seeing truth. Without compassion, truth feels threatening; with it, truth becomes workable.

Examples

He uses the story of Khidr and the Land of Truth: some choose happiness, others suffering, but the decisive point is to desire truth for its own sake.

First inquiry: where do I prefer consolation to a truth that might liberate me?

08

Trust

Diamond Heart I, chapter 8.

What Almaas Takes Up

Trust in oneself, another person, the group, a truth, and the fear of becoming vulnerable.

The Shift of Vision

Trust is not naivete and not a guarantee of being protected. It is the capacity to open to what is here without immediately defending. It is related to truth and compassion.

Examples

Students speak of safety, deserving trust, vulnerability, and trusting oneself. Almaas distinguishes several levels: practical trust, trust in another's intention, and deeper trust in the reality of experience.

First inquiry: what arises when I actually begin to trust: relaxation, fear, withdrawal, need for proof?

09

Essence Is the Life

Diamond Heart I, chapter 9.

What Almaas Takes Up

The desire to get something from spiritual work, and projection: seeing outside what we cannot yet recognize inside.

The Shift of Vision

Fulfillment is not getting what personality wants. It is being Essence. Yet we often project Essence onto a beloved person, a teacher, a situation, or an ideal.

Examples

He moves from negative projection, such as fear or hostility attributed to others, to positive projection: falling in love because the other seems to carry our own love, value, or essential beauty.

First inquiry: what beauty I see outside might be a quality of my Essence I do not yet dare to recognize?

10

The Value of Struggling

Diamond Heart I, chapter 10.

What Almaas Takes Up

The wish that the teacher solve our problems, give us an answer, relieve us, or make life easier.

The Shift of Vision

Struggle is not a failure of the path. It is the digestion of the work itself. An answer received too quickly does not become knowledge; friction with the problem roots realization.

Examples

He compares struggle to digestion, internal combustion, making wine, and concentrating juice. Difficulties become intelligent roadblocks that force us back toward ourselves.

First inquiry: what problem do I want to delegate when it is asking me to develop my own perception?

11

Truth

Diamond Heart I, chapter 11.

What Almaas Takes Up

The phrase “truth liberates,” moral honesty, facts, insights, and the difference between saying something accurate and acting from the whole truth.

The Shift of Vision

Truth is not merely a correct fact. It has an inner taste: certainty, freedom, intimacy with oneself. Relative truth is alive when it carries the fragrance of deeper truth.

Examples

He uses concrete cases: hiding someone in danger, whether to speak to a sister who cannot hear, distinguishing factual accuracy from action attuned to the whole situation.

First inquiry: am I stating a fact to be right, or serving the truth of the whole situation?

12

Allowing

Diamond Heart I, chapter 12.

What Almaas Takes Up

Our ideas about growth: becoming happier, more lovable, more successful, keeping the same identity but improving it.

The Shift of Vision

Transformation means becoming other than what we imagined. Allowing is neither forcing nor remaining passive: it lets the real process open possibilities personality cannot conceive.

Examples

He plays with the image of larva and butterfly, then with deliberately unexpected images: fruit, tree, planet, star. The point is that Essence unfolds beyond our larval projects.

First inquiry: what image of my transformation is already limiting what might reveal itself?

13

Growing Up

Diamond Heart I, chapter 13.

What Almaas Takes Up

Complaints, expectations toward a partner or teacher, and the need for someone to give us love, safety, esteem, or support.

The Shift of Vision

Growing up is not becoming cold or defensively independent. It is ceasing to ask the world to be mother or father, and discovering in Essence the support, love, and reality that cannot be obtained outside.

Examples

He compares society to an unrecognized nursery, shows complaint as a call to mother, and reads ordinary relationships as repetitions of infantile demands.

First inquiry: whom am I still asking to give me what only Essence can reveal in me?

14

The Student's Relationship to the Teaching

Diamond Heart I, chapter 14.

What Almaas Takes Up

The way we treat inner work: side activity, authority to resist, resource to consume, or real basis of transformation.

The Shift of Vision

Our relationship to the teaching reflects our relationship to Essence. Resistances, objections, and projections are not to be condemned but understood: they show where personality defends its world.

Examples

He speaks of time, energy, money, rules, reactions to the teacher, unconscious loyalty to parents and conditioning. For a site reader, the question becomes: what real place does truth have in my life?

First inquiry: do I treat practice as an addition, or as a basis that can transform my life?

15

The Impeccable Warrior

Diamond Heart I, chapter 15.

What Almaas Takes Up

Complacency: knowing what is true and then falling back into the same pattern through weakness, distraction, comfort, or uncertainty.

The Shift of Vision

Impeccability is not perfectionism. It means actually doing one's best with what one knows now. It supports the healthy part against complacency without becoming punishment.

Examples

He takes very ordinary examples: choosing again the person who rejects, staying busy to avoid, arriving late because of dessert, working or not working with emotions and superego.

First inquiry: where do I know enough to act, but let uncertainty protect my complacency?

16

Curiosity

Diamond Heart I, chapter 16.

What Almaas Takes Up

The spiritual search itself: avoiding pain, seeking pleasure, and even seeking truth as a new way to get something.

The Shift of Vision

Curiosity is not oriented toward a result. It is the spirit of truth in the heart, the living movement that looks at what is here because it is worthy of being known.

Examples

He comments on a story attributed to Jesus: two groups remain caught in pain and pleasure; the third is not moved by a goal but by the spirit of truth. He compares this to children's spontaneous curiosity.

First inquiry: am I seeking truth to obtain a state, or because present experience calls me?

17

Gathering Honey

Diamond Heart I, chapter 17.

What Almaas Takes Up

The difference between information and living knowledge, and the role of a school or group in concentrating Essence.

The Shift of Vision

Spiritual knowledge is not only what we read. It is distilled in the human being. The image of honey points to material, concentrated knowledge gathered through the work of many.

Examples

He uses bees, nectars, the hive, human vessels, and physical activities done in presence. Transmission becomes embodied practice, not the accumulation of ideas.

First inquiry: does what I learn remain information, or is it becoming a substance of presence in my life?

Provisional Conclusion

This first series is more radical than a simple summary suggests. It does not simply offer a few famous themes; it teaches us to recognize how any ordinary situation can be a distorted reflection, a doorway, or a defense before Essence. This logic also guides the second series.
