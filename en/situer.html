Placing Almaas

Where he comes from, whom to compare him with, what is original — and what must be faced squarely.

A teacher of inner experience

What should we call Almaas? The sources consulted support two simple facts: Hameed Ali comes from scientific and psychological training, then founds and teaches the Diamond Approach within the Ridhwan school. They do not place him as a university professor of philosophy, nor as the master of an established religious tradition. The most accurate wording is therefore: a contemporary mystical thinker, a spiritual teacher and a phenomenologist of inner experience — someone who describes, with a precision nourished by his scientific training, what happens in consciousness when one explores it. His work nonetheless builds a metaphysics, an anthropology of consciousness and a psychology of the ego.

The sources — and his own position

His teaching draws on materials from everywhere: the personality/essence contrast inherited from Gurdjieff; the enneagram transmitted by Ichazo and Naranjo; the lataif and the soul of Sufism; the emptiness and nonduality of Buddhism; the Vedānta's quest for the Self; object-relations psychoanalysis (Mahler, Winnicott, Kohut, Kernberg, Balint, Guntrip); Reich's body work.

Yet his own position must be reported honestly: Almaas rejects the idea of a deliberate “blend” of traditions. In his account, the teaching unfolded from his experiential investigations — the traditions' concepts mainly helped him understand and formulate what he was discovering. That is his spiritual interpretation; a historian would not treat it as a demonstrable fact.

Source markers

This page must clearly distinguish three statuses: what Almaas writes himself, what close students or scholars formulate about him, and the comparisons proposed by this site.

The links with Sufism and Ibn ʿArabī rest on real occurrences in the local corpus, especially The Pearl Beyond Price, The Point of Existence, The Inner Journey Home, Facets of Unity, Spacecruiser Inquiry and Runaway Realization. Their scope varies from book to book: they attest to quotations, echoes, and comparisons, not to Almaas's belonging to a classical Sufi doctrine.

When the site relies on John Davis, Ann Gleig or Harry Hunt, these are secondary or critical sources, useful for placing the work, but distinct from Almaas's own books.

The discipline this site must keep

Placing such a cross-traditional work requires particular care. Many comparisons are tempting because they sound right: Sufism, Buddhism, Vedānta, phenomenology, psychoanalysis, the Fourth Way. But a useful comparison is not yet a source. The site must therefore keep a simple rule: when a statement concerns Almaas himself, it should come from his books, his interviews, or identifiable secondary sources. When it is the site's pedagogical reading, it should be presented as such.

1What is attested

His scientific training, his participation in Naranjo's SAT group, the founding of the Ridhwan school, the importance of Gurdjieff, Ichazo, and Naranjo for the enneagram, the use of certain Sufi notions, and several explicit references to Ibn ʿArabī all belong to the consulted corpus. These elements can be mentioned confidently, but without turning points of contact into a complete doctrinal lineage.

2What is comparison

Saying that a notion in Almaas recalls Ibn ʿArabī, Vedānta, or Buddhism can help the reader, but it is not the same as saying that it directly derives from them. Comparison should illuminate, not replace the source.

3What must remain open

Some proximities are real yet difficult to settle historically. The site can then use careful wording: “in dialogue with,” “recalls,” “can be compared with,” “without identifying it with.” This care protects the beauty of the work: it avoids making it say more than it says.

Sufism: close, but free

The Sufi imprint is real: certain Arabic terms, the lataif, the language of the heart, references to Rūmī, Idries Shah, or Ibn ʿArabī, and a sensitivity to the inner path run through several books. But this does not make Almaas the master of a tariqa: his teaching requires no adherence to Islam, and he also relates Essence to Buddha-nature, the Ātman, the Tao, or the Spirit of the Abrahamic traditions. His work belongs to a contemporary trans-religious spirituality — in dialogue with classical Sufism, never to be confused with it.

Ibn ʿArabī: real source, careful comparison

Ibn ʿArabī is not a decorative comparison added from outside: Almaas cites him and sometimes compares him with other traditions, especially around the unity of Being, manifestation, the continual renewal of creation, and certain ways of understanding the path. Other possible comparisons — for instance with the doctrine of the Perfect Man or a complete doctrine of the soul — should remain presented as interpretive unless they are sourced passage by passage. For Ibn ʿArabī, true existence belongs to God alone, and creatures receive their being as loci of manifestation of the divine Names, within a universe structured by the Qur'an and prophecy. For Almaas, reality is likewise one, and his essential aspects can recall divine qualities by analogy: strength, compassion, truth, will, love. But they are neither a systematic restatement of the divine Names nor a simple psychological translation of Ibn ʿArabī. The decisive difference is one of method and language: Almaas starts from present individual experience, in a phenomenological and psychological idiom. Bringing them together is fruitful; equating them would be lazy.

What is original

Almaas's originality lies less in any single idea than in their articulation: developmental psychology and nondual mysticism; childhood wounds and essential qualities; individuation and transcendence of the ego; the lived body and absolute consciousness. The scholar Ann Gleig, in her article on the Diamond Approach's mystical humanism, helps name this orientation: a spirituality in which the personal human is not eliminated but clarified, integrated into nondual reality. The psychologist Harry Hunt, in a transpersonal and psychoanalytic reading of Almaas, likewise points to the precision with which the work connects presence, the Fourth Way, Sufi psychology, object relations and Reichian work.

Endless enlightenment — and humility

Almaas distinguishes realization — recognizing true nature and abiding in it, by degrees — from complete enlightenment, permanent and without obscurations. Asked directly, he explicitly declares that he does not consider himself fully enlightened by that demanding definition. Hence his phrase “endless enlightenment”: no experience constitutes the conclusion of the path. In the founder of a school, that particular humility is not so common.

An honest look

This site loves this work — all the more reason to place it without complacency. Three levels must be distinguished:

The phenomenological description — often remarkably fine: this is the verifiable core, each in one's own experience.

The psychological interpretation — fruitful, but not universally validated by research.

The spiritual metaphysics — essential aspects, Guidance, dimensions, the Absolute: claims grounded in contemplative experience, matters of examination and assent, not of scientific proof.

Let us add: the Ridhwan school asks for a long and costly commitment, as Almaas himself acknowledges; and the academic literature on his work remains limited. None of this makes his thought insignificant — it simply invites us to read it as it asks to be read: by verifying in one's own experience.

Editorial rule to keep: never make Almaas the official representative of a tradition. Name him instead as a contemporary teacher of the Diamond Approach, in dialogue with several inheritances while reorganizing them from experience.
