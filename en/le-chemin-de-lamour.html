The path of love

Pink, gold, pomegranate — the faces of love, all the way to the inner Beloved.

Love is not an emotion

We speak of love as one feeling among others — a happy mood, an attachment, a surge that comes and goes. Almaas proposes something else: love is not primarily what one feels about someone, it is a presence — as real and palpable as a warmth in the chest, a density, a precise texture one can recognize in the body, independent of its object.

This is his usual method: not to philosophize about love, but to let it show itself as it is actually lived — and to discover that it does not show itself in only one way. Love has colors. Almaas distinguishes three, within the register of personal love, the love lived between two beings: pink, melting gold, pomegranate. Each has its own flavor, its function, its distortions — and each deserves to be recognized on its own terms, without being confused with the other two.

The three colors of personal love

These three qualities are not opposed to one another and form no hierarchy: they follow one another, blend, and return at different moments within the same relationship.

PinkThe appreciation that loves without need

This is the simplest sweetness to recognize: the pure pleasure of seeing the other, of appreciating what they are, wanting nothing more. A tenderness that is not hungry — it does not seek to fill a lack, it is content to savor the singular beauty of the person loved. Almaas compares it to an almost childlike sweetness: light, sugary, weightless.

It is also the least mature of the three when it stands alone: as long as it depends on an idealized image of the other, it shatters at the first disappointment. Its maturity comes when it widens into a love able to embrace the other in their entirety — qualities and flaws alike — without the bond breaking.

GoldThe fusion that dissolves separation

Another flavor, more enveloping: the desire for closeness, to be pressed right up against, to no longer feel the boundary between self and other. Directly experienced, this quality has the texture of a golden, transparent, almost syrupy nectar — it melts away the body's tensions and the sense of being separate, without erasing who one is.

Almaas links this desire for fusion to life's earliest experience: the original unity between the infant and the one who carries it. Every adult story of fusion still bears its trace. But melting gold is not a regression: it is what reveals itself when one stays present to the void of separation, instead of hurrying to fill it.

PomegranateThe passion that consumes and unifies

The most intense of the three: a burning love that does not settle for drawing near, but drives toward union through complete dissolution. Almaas names it after the pomegranate fruit — dense, gorged, ready to burst. It is a passion that consumes the boundaries of the self rather than working around them, and that unifies precisely because it recoils from nothing it encounters.

It is also the most often held back: fear of its own intensity, old wounds around desire, restrain the pomegranate before it can even unfold. To recover it does not mean acting on it without measure: it means ceasing to fear it.

Desire reclaimed

Spiritual tradition is often wary of desire: it is seen as the obstacle, the thing to be tamed to reach a purer love. In The Power of Divine Eros, written with his longtime collaborator Karen Johnson, Almaas takes the opposite view: at their root, generous love and burning desire are not two rival forces. They are one and the same energy — which the ego's self-centered experience has split in two.

This book grew out of a seminar grounded, very concretely, in the body: an attention brought to the center of the belly, seat of the instinctual energies — of survival, sociability, sexuality. The central idea is summed up in an image that Almaas and Johnson return to throughout the text: it is a matter of uniting the animal and the angel — not denying our instinctual nature to earn the sacred, but recognizing that desire, fully felt rather than acted out in haste, is already the soul's own movement toward its truth.

When love no longer has an object

The three colors above are lived between two beings: there is oneself, there is the other, and love flows between them. In Nondual Love, Almaas describes a different register — not a stronger love, but a love that no longer needs a recipient. A sweetness, a goodness that is directed at nothing because it already pervades everything, including the one who believes they are feeling it.

What most often prevents us from tasting it, Almaas says, is a quiet belief: that there must be someone who has an experience, a separate "self" who owns their love the way one owns a possession. As long as that belief holds, love stays locked within the relationship between two. When it loosens, even for a moment, love reveals itself as the very nature of what is — with no subject who loves, no object loved, and yet nothing is missing.

The Inner Beloved

One last turn of the path remains, perhaps the oldest: that of the mystics, of Sufi poets like Rumi, for whom all human love is never more than the reflection of a vaster love, addressed — at bottom — to a single Beloved. In The Inner Beloved, Almaas takes up this devotional path of the heart and retraces it with the same demand for direct experience that runs through his entire body of work.

The ordinary heart, he says, loves in a kind of "polygamy": scattered among multiple objects — people, things, ideals — never finding complete satisfaction. This is not a flaw to correct: it is the starting point of a path. Little by little, as the heart strips away its successive attachments — what Almaas calls the poverty of the heart — and stays faithful to what it truly loves, without the mind imposing a direction, it comes to recognize that a single love has always been there, beneath all the others: that of the inner Beloved. Not an outward figure to worship, but the most intimate nature of what we are — recognized, at last, as worthy of love.

Exploration

Three loves, three colors

Think back to three loves in your life — it doesn't matter whether they lasted a day or several decades, whether they concern a person, a place, a work.

  1. For each, let the sensation come, more than the memory: was it light and appreciative — more pink? Enveloping, fusional — more gold? Intense, devouring — more pomegranate?
  2. The same love may have changed color over time, or blended several at once: simply note it, without trying to decide.
  3. None of the three colors is superior to the others. The question is not which one you succeeded at best — but which you recognize most easily, and which still feels, for now, more foreign.
