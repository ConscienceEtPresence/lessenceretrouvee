Step III

The holes

The lack has exactly the shape of what is missing. Based on the founding teaching of the Approach.

What a hole is

There are places within us that seem empty. Not empty like a clear sky: empty like a room from which one particular piece of furniture has been removed, whose shape you can still feel in the space it left behind. Almaas calls this a hole : the felt absence of a quality of being we have lost the ability to touch. Where there was strength, a dull powerlessness; where there was worth, a shame that dares not speak its name; where there was love, a heart that feels dry, like wood. These are not metaphors : they are locatable sensations, often in the body itself — a hollow in the chest, a void behind the solar plexus, a flatness that follows certain gestures.

The essential point, the one that changes everything : the quality is not destroyed. It has never left the premises. The hole is not proof of absence : it is the negative imprint, in relief, of a presence we have stopped perceiving. We do not manufacture the strength that is missing — we recover access to a strength that, itself, never stopped being there.

◆ ◆ ◆

Why we flee from them

A hole, fully felt, is uncomfortable — sometimes vertiginous. So we spend most of our energy making sure we never meet it. We fill it with activity, with a schedule that leaves no moment of silence. We fill it with food, with screens, with distractions that fill the instant without ever passing through it. We fill it, too, with relationships : we look in another person for exactly the quality we lack — their strength for our powerlessness, their warmth for our dryness — and in this way we build bonds that are, without our knowing it, bandages. None of this is a fault. It is an ancient strategy, learned very early, and often the only one available to the child we once were. But a permanent bandage ends up preventing the very healing it was meant to protect.

Two kinds of emptiness we confuse

A precise distinction is needed here, one Almaas develops at length in The Void : not all emptiness is alike, even when it looks alike on the surface. There is deficient emptiness — that depressive, alienating void that psychoanalysis describes : the feeling of not being real, of being cut off from oneself and others, of having a hole where a center should be. And there is space — a quality of being itself, light, clear, open, that appears when a rigid self-image dissolves. The two can feel almost identical at first glance. But one starves, the other liberates. One closes in on itself in a spiral; the other opens, like a room from which a wall has suddenly been taken away.

The difference cannot be seen from outside : it is felt by staying. This is why flight is so tempting — because on entering the void, we cannot know in advance which of the two we will meet. The only way to find out is to stay long enough for the sensation to reveal itself.

◆ ◆ ◆

The turning point : staying with the hole

This is the discovery on which the whole Approach is founded : the hole is not the enemy. There is nothing to repair, nothing to forcibly fill, nothing to defeat. It is enough — which does not mean it is easy — to turn toward it and stay there, without plugging it, without interpreting it, without rushing to get out of it.

In practice, it looks like this : you notice the sensation of lack — often when some situation stirs it, a refusal, a comparison, a silence that goes on too long. Instead of covering it up right away (with a thought, an activity, a justification), you take a moment to locate it in the body : where exactly is it felt? What shape does it have? Then you stay with it, with the gentleness you would offer a frightened child — without demanding that anything change, without turning attention into a new task to succeed at. No heroism is required here : the only thing asked is not to leave right away.

What happens then

What follows cannot be forced, cannot be predicted in detail, and resembles no method that could be mechanically repeated. But a fairly constant movement, does emerge : the void, held without fleeing, ceases to be a lack and becomes a space. And within that space, the quality it signaled has a way of presenting itself on its own — not summoned, not manufactured, simply returned, like a light rediscovered on entering a room where, without knowing it, we had left the switch on. The lack was not an absence : it was a door, and it had been opening, all along, in the direction we did not dare to push.

Exploration — 15 to 20 minutes

Staying with a hole

Sit quietly, somewhere you will not be disturbed.

  1. Notice a lack that recurs often in your life — a familiar feeling, almost a signature (“I'm not good enough”, “no one really sees me”, “I'm not allowed to rest”). Choose just one.
  2. Let that feeling arise, then look for where it settles in the body. Don't think the answer : feel it. A tight chest, a hollow stomach, a knotted throat, a void behind the eyes.
  3. Stay there for several minutes, doing nothing but feeling — without analyzing it, without searching for its cause, without trying to make it go away. If the mind wanders off into a story or an explanation, gently bring attention back to the sensation itself.
  4. At the end, note what has changed — even slightly : a texture shifting, a warmth, a release, or simply the fact of having held steady without fleeing. Nothing has to happen on the first day : the practice is built through repetition, not performance.

Return to this exploration with the same lack several times over the weeks that follow, before choosing another. A familiar hole rarely reveals itself all at once.
