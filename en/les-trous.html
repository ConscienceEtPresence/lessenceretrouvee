Door III

The holes

Lack can point to the quality that has become inaccessible. Based on the founding teaching of the Approach.

What a hole is

There are places within us that seem empty. Not empty like a clear sky: empty like a room from which one particular piece of furniture has been removed, whose shape you can still feel in the space it left behind. Almaas calls this a hole : the felt absence of a quality of being we have lost the ability to touch. Where there was strength, a dull powerlessness; where there was worth, a shame that dares not speak its name; where there was love, a heart that feels dry, like wood. These are not metaphors : they are locatable sensations, often in the body itself — a hollow in the chest, a void behind the solar plexus, a flatness that follows certain gestures.

The essential point, the one that changes everything : the quality is not destroyed. It has never left the premises. The hole is not proof of absence : it is the negative imprint, in relief, of a presence we have stopped perceiving. We do not manufacture the strength that is missing — we recover access to a strength that, itself, never stopped being there.

The deeper schema

In Almaas's language, then, a hole is not simply a character “defect”. It is an event in consciousness: an essential quality is no longer accessible, the soul feels this disconnection as deficiency, and the personality tries to organize a substitute solution. The false filling can be very subtle, sometimes even socially valued. One can appear strong in order not to feel powerlessness, brilliant in order not to feel confusion, generous in order not to feel the need for love.

This movement explains why some efforts at transformation remain sterile. If we only add a new spiritual image on top of the hole, one filling replaces another. If we are willing to feel the deficiency itself, the work becomes more real: the false solution relaxes, and the quality it imitated can reveal itself.

Why we flee from them

A hole, fully felt, is uncomfortable — sometimes vertiginous. It may also be wrapped in shame, fear, anger, self-images, superego attacks, or bodily tensions. So we spend most of our energy making sure we never meet it. We fill it with activity, with a schedule that leaves no moment of silence. We fill it with food, with screens, with distractions that fill the instant without ever passing through it. We fill it, too, with relationships : we look in another person for exactly the quality we lack — their strength for our powerlessness, their warmth for our dryness — and in this way we build bonds that are, without our knowing it, bandages. None of this is a fault. It is an ancient strategy, learned very early, and often the only one available to the child we once were. But a permanent bandage ends up preventing the very healing it was meant to protect.

False fillings

To make the theory truly concrete, we need to look at how a hole gets filled. Filling is not always crude. It can look like success, morality, a relational style, a temperament or even “spiritual practice”. Almaas emphasizes this point: the Work itself can become a way not to feel the hole, if we use it to become someone special or to avoid vulnerability.

External confirmation

The hole of value seeks approval, admiration, success or a validating gaze. Relief comes, but it depends on others.

Relational projection

We ask another person to carry the strength, love or support we believe we lack. Relationship becomes a bandage.

Pseudo-feelings

Superiority, anger, coldness, control or excitement can mask shame, hurt, emptiness or powerlessness.

Rigid identity

A belief about oneself replaces direct experience: “I am strong”, “I am worthless”, “I am above this”.

Spirituality used

Inner work can become a filling if one seeks an awakened self-image instead of the truth of the moment.

Constant agitation

Busyness, screens, food, projects or emergencies prevent us from meeting the emptiness that wants to show itself.

Two kinds of emptiness we confuse

A precise distinction is needed here, one Almaas develops at length in The Void : not all emptiness is alike, even when it looks alike on the surface. There is deficient emptiness — that depressive, alienating void that psychoanalysis describes : the feeling of not being real, of being cut off from oneself and others, of having a hole where a center should be. And there is space — a quality of being itself, light, clear, open, that appears when a rigid self-image dissolves. The two can feel almost identical at first glance. But one starves, the other liberates. One closes in on itself in a spiral; the other opens, like a room from which a wall has suddenly been taken away.

The difference cannot be seen from outside : it is felt by staying. This is why flight is so tempting — because on entering the void, we cannot know in advance which of the two we will meet. The only way to find out is to stay long enough for the sensation to reveal itself.

Do not confuse

Hole: the felt absence of an essential quality, lived as lack or deficiency.

Space: a positive openness of Being, often discovered when a self-image dissolves.

Absolute emptiness: a much more radical dimension, not a psychological lack.

The turning point : staying with the hole

This is the discovery on which the whole Approach is founded : the hole is not the enemy. There is nothing to repair by willpower, nothing to forcibly fill, nothing to defeat. But this is not a simple mechanism : turning toward it requires presence, understanding, and sometimes support. One stays with it without plugging it, without interpreting it too fast, without rushing to get out of it.

In practice, it looks like this : you notice the sensation of lack — often when some situation stirs it, a refusal, a comparison, a silence that goes on too long. Instead of covering it up right away (with a thought, an activity, a justification), you take a moment to locate it in the body : where exactly is it felt? What shape does it have? Then you stay with it, with the gentleness you would offer a frightened child — without demanding that anything change, without turning attention into a new task to succeed at. No heroism is required here : the only thing asked is not to leave right away.

What happens then

What follows cannot be forced, cannot be predicted in detail, and resembles no method that could be mechanically repeated. The void may first reveal infant pain, a defense, an object relation, a fear, or an empty space. But a fairly constant movement does emerge : when the hole is explored with enough presence and understanding, it can cease to be only a lack and become a space. In that space, the quality it signaled may present itself — not summoned, not manufactured, but recognized again. The lack was not a simple absence : it could be a door.

The cycle: awakening, death, rebirth

The notes on Essence and The Elixir describe a very useful cycle for reading this process. First there is awakening: something becomes conscious in a sector of the personality. We see, for example, that our need to be admired is not merely vanity, but an attempt to support a value we no longer feel. Then comes a kind of death: the false structure no longer convinces, and we meet the emptiness it was holding at bay. Finally there may be rebirth: not the old strategy refurbished, but the emergence of the corresponding essential quality.

This cycle is not mechanical. We cannot decide: “today I cross the hole and tomorrow the quality appears”. But it offers a precious orientation: when a defense loses its force and emptiness appears, it is not necessarily a failure. It may be the exact passage where the work becomes deeper.

Exploration — 15 to 20 minutes

Staying with a hole

Sit quietly, somewhere you will not be disturbed.

  1. Notice a lack that recurs often in your life — a familiar feeling, almost a signature (“I'm not good enough”, “no one really sees me”, “I'm not allowed to rest”). Choose just one.
  2. Let that feeling arise, then look for where it settles in the body. Don't think the answer : feel it. A tight chest, a hollow stomach, a knotted throat, a void behind the eyes.
  3. Stay there for several minutes, doing nothing but feeling — without analyzing it, without searching for its cause, without trying to make it go away. If the mind wanders off into a story or an explanation, gently bring attention back to the sensation itself.
  4. At the end, note what has changed — even slightly : a texture shifting, a warmth, a release, or simply the fact of having held steady without fleeing. Nothing has to happen on the first day : the practice is built through repetition, not performance.

Return to this exploration with the same lack several times over the weeks that follow, before choosing another. A familiar hole rarely reveals itself all at once.

Going further · after Essence and Sexuality

The genital hole

In his very first work — his 1983 thesis, more technical than the rest of the œuvre — Almaas describes a hole that is at once very concrete and very general: the "genital hole." It is a sense of emptiness in the pelvis that arises, like all holes, from the loss of contact with essence in childhood. His bold claim: this emptiness is the real substrate of what Freud called the castration complex — in men and women alike.

Sexuality bears its trace. Almaas links it to two essential qualities perceived from the cradle: the Red (strength, excitation) and the Gold (merging, sweetness), whose blend forms the libido. He also describes negative merging: to protect love in a deepening couple, one often represses, unknowingly, one's Red energy — and thereby puts out desire itself.

The point: even the most bodily lack obeys the same law as every hole. To go through it, rather than fill it, reopens access to the living quality it covers over.
